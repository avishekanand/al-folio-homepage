Why are we here?

Concrete questions we keep hearing on campus

“Can we have an endpoint that students can use in my lecture of 400 students for learning to code with LLMs?”

“Can we use LLM infra for annotating data in my research, because OpenAI is too expensive?”

“I don’t want to send resumes of PhD candidates to a commercial LLM — data privacy.”

The pattern

These aren’t asks for a chatbot. They are asks for shared, governed, affordable inference that an institution can stand behind.

These three quotes are paraphrased from real conversations on campus over recent months: from course coordinators dealing with cohort-scale tooling, from research groups whose annotation costs on commercial APIs are unsustainable, and from faculty handling sensitive personal data.

They surfaced independently from different parts of TU Delft, and they all point to the same gap: serving infrastructure that the institution provides, rather than each project arranging its own.

LLMs are becoming infrastructure

Researchers and educators are already using LLMs through APIs

Common patterns today:

  • chat interfaces
  • coding assistants (Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code)
  • RAG over internal documents
  • agentic workflows
  • research prototypes and educational tools

Most of these applications do not run models directly. They consume model endpoints provided by external services.

Most LLM use at TU Delft already happens through APIs, not through models that individuals run themselves. The serving layer is therefore the layer most people implicitly depend on, even when they don’t think of it that way.

LLM serving ≠ LLM experimentation

Two different questions, two different infrastructures


LLM experimentation LLM serving
Fine-tuning models Reliable inference
Benchmarking Stable endpoints
Trying new architectures Multi-user access
One researcher Hundreds of users
GPU allocation API management
Accuracy focus Reliability focus

The framing

  • Research asks: can we build a better model?
  • Serving asks: can hundreds of users reliably use a model?

This distinction is often blurred. The TU Delft AI conversation defaults to “research compute” — DAIC, DelftBlue, GPU allocation — but TULIP lives on the serving side: not which model is best, but how a chosen model is made reliably available to many users, scripts, and tools.

Why universities need LLM serving — Accessibility

Shared access to AI capabilities

Where shared access matters

  • students without paid subscriptions
  • teaching at scale
  • consistent model access across cohorts
  • shared infrastructure for groups
  • reproducible research environments

Concrete examples

  • course assistants
  • coding support in classrooms
  • shared research prototypes
  • student projects on stable endpoints

The accessibility argument

Today the quality of a student’s or researcher’s AI access often depends on whether they can pay for a personal subscription. A serving layer flattens this.

The accessibility pillar is about who can use AI capabilities at all, and under what conditions. Right now this is heavily individualised: people who can afford subscriptions or have grants get good access; others don’t. A shared institutional layer changes that baseline.

This matters most in teaching — a course can’t depend on every student having a personal API key — and in shared research tools where the dependency needs to be predictable.

Why universities need LLM serving — Governance

Institutional control over AI usage

Where governance matters

  • data remains within TU Delft
  • GDPR compliance
  • controlled access and authentication
  • auditable usage
  • reduced dependence on external providers

Concrete examples

  • student assessments and feedback
  • interviews and human-subject data
  • internal and unpublished documents
  • sensitive research data

The governance argument

Without institutional serving, sensitive workflows fall back to shadow IT (personal accounts on external APIs) or no AI at all.

Governance is the pillar that universities most readily understand because it ties to existing policy: GDPR, research-data management, contracts with external providers, and institutional liability.

The practical risk is not abstract. When local serving is unavailable, researchers and educators either route sensitive data through commercial APIs (often without an institutional agreement) or avoid LLMs entirely. Both are bad outcomes.

Why universities need LLM serving — Institutional capability

Endpoints that outlive grants, subscriptions, and projects

Where capability matters

  • universities increasingly build AI-powered systems
  • RAG platforms over institutional content
  • research software and pipelines
  • educational tools
  • agentic workflows

Concrete examples

  • shared research tools
  • durable educational infrastructure
  • prototypes that survive personnel changes
  • platforms used across departments

The strategic point

An LLM serving layer isn’t just a convenience for individual researchers — it is a piece of institutional infrastructure that the university needs to own, the same way it owns DAIC, NetID, or DelftBlue.

The capability pillar is the most forward-looking. Universities are no longer just users of AI; they are builders of AI-powered systems. Every one of those systems needs a model endpoint that persists.

If those endpoints come from external providers, the university’s systems are coupled to commercial roadmaps, pricing changes, and terms of service. An institutional serving layer is what makes long-lived TU Delft systems possible.

This is the natural lead-in to TULIP: a concrete attempt to provide that durable inference layer.

What is TULIP?

TU Delft LLM Inference Platform

A REIT initiative, generously hosted by TU Delft ICT

TULIP is not a model and not just a chatbot.

It is an API-first local inference layer: a way for TU Delft researchers, educators, scripts, notebooks, tools, and applications to access hosted LLM capabilities through stable interfaces.

The point of today is not to present TULIP as a finished campus service, but to understand what it needs to become useful.

What is available now?


Capability Current status
Models Chat, code, embeddings
Access Controlled pilot users
Interface OpenAI-compatible API endpoints
Integration Scripts, notebooks, IDE tools, compatible clients, and agentic workflows/tools
Operations GPU, latency, queue, and service monitoring
Scale Small platform: 1 server, 3× L40S GPUs

Current status

TULIP is a controlled pilot-scale platform, not yet a full campus-wide production service.

With thanks to TU Delft ICT

The server node and L40S GPUs that TULIP runs on are generously hosted by TU Delft ICT. The platform exists because of their infrastructure support.
